Raewyn (formerly R.W.) Connell (1944-) is recognised as being one of the most important contributors to sociological thought in Australia. As you read in Chapter 1, results from a 2003 survey of Australian sociologists identified Connell's Ruling Class, Ruling Culture (1977) as the most influential book in Australian sociology (Skrbis and Germov 2004).

Connell's (1977) analysis of class relations in Australia focused on 'the structure and internal complexity of the Australian ruling class, the way class relations influence political events, and the impact of class on ideology, culture and consciousness' (Western and Western 1990: 65).

The basic premise of this thesis was that class relations impact all other social relations. For Connell, though, a discussion of the ruling class couldn't be confined to an analysis of private ownership and capital accumulation. Rather, to understand the ruling class it was deemed necessary to look in detail at ruling class activityâ€”activity which involves 'the exercise of power and control that is embodied in profit making, corporate growth and ruling class mobilisation and resistance to working class politics' (Western and Western 1990: 65).

The working class is largely absent from this exploration of class, and this silence is linked to the ideological domination of the ruling class. InConnell's view, the media plays a significant role in the cultural domination because it produces 'a set of middle class cultural categories which imply a definite interpretation of the way the world operates' (Western and Western 1990: 66).
